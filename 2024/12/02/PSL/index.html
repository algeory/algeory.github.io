Projective special linear group of finite field

Posted on 2024-12-02

1.\(GL(n,q)\) is the group of matrices whose determinants are not zero,and \(a_{ij}\in F_q\) ,\(|GL(n,q)|=\prod \limits _0^{q-1} q^n-q^i\) Suppose \(M \in GL(n,q)\), the \(n\) row vectors of \(M\) :\(\alpha_1 ,\alpha_2, \cdots \alpha_n\)are linearly independent,and \(\alpha_i=(a_1,a_2,\cdots ,a_n)^T,a_i\in F_q\). Since the numbers in \(\alpha_1\) can be chosen freely except \((0,0,\cdots,0)\), \(\alpha_1\) has \(q^n-1\) choices. As soon as \(\alpha_1\) is decided, \(\alpha_2\) need to be indepent with \(\alpha_1\) ,so it can not be \(0\cdot \alpha_1,1\cdot \alpha_1,\cdots,(q-1)\cdot \alpha_1\) ,so \(\alpha_2\) has \(q^n-q\) choices. Similarly,\(\alpha_3\) has \(q^n-q^2\) choices,\(\cdots\) ,\(\alpha_n\) has \(q^n-q^{n-1}\) choices Totally,\(GL(n,q)\) has \(\prod \limits _0^{q-1} q^n-q^i\) elements.

2.\(SL(n,q)\) is the group of matrices whose determinant is one,and \(a_{ij}\in F_q\) ,\(|SL(n,q)|=\frac{|GL(n,q)|}{q-1}=\frac{\prod \limits _0^{q-1} q^n-q^i}{q-1}\) Since all the determinant are in \(F_q^*\),the number of matrixs having the determinant \(1,2,\cdots q-1\) should be same ,intuitively, and it does.

3.\(SL(n,q)\) is the equivalent classes by quotient the scalar multiplicative in \(SL(n,q)\) \(A\sim B\) when \(A=kB=kI_nB\) ,and since \(1=det(A)=det(kB)=k^ndet(B)=k^n\) there are finitely \(k\) making this equivalence happen.And since the number of solution of the equation \(x^n=e\) in \(F_q^*\) is \((n,q-1)\),the are \((n,q-1)\) different equivalent calsses. Thus \(|SL(n,q)|=\frac{|SL(n,q)|}{\sim}=\frac{|SL(n,q)|}{(n,q-1)}\)

4.The number of solutions of the equation \(x^n=e\) in \(F_q^*\) Since \(F_q\) is finite field ,so \(F_q^*\) is a cyclic group ,denote the generator be \(g\) ,we have \(g^{q-1}=e\) The equation turns to be \(g^{kn}=e\) ,since the order of \(g\) is \(q-1\) ,the is a dividing relation \(q-1|kn\) . The number of solutions is the number of \(k\)'s making the relation happen. The smallest \(k\) is \(\frac{q-1}{(n,q-1)}\) ,and the largest \(k\) is \(\frac{(q-1)(n,q-1)}{(n,q-1)}\),so there are \((n,q-1)\) \(k\)'s ,and the equation has \((n,q-1)\) solutions.
